About This Section

Why Study the Greek New Testament?

The New Testament was written in Koine Greek — the common language of the first-century Mediterranean world. While faithful English translations convey the meaning of Scripture reliably, studying the original language opens a deeper window into the text: word meanings that carry theological weight, verb tenses that reveal aspect and action, and syntactical structures that shape interpretation.

For students of Free Grace theology in particular, precision in the Greek text matters greatly. Key terms like πιστεύω (to believe), ζωή αίώνιος (eternal life), and σῳζω (to save) carry precise meanings that inform debates around the gospel, assurance, and sanctification.

Recommended Resources

Going Deeper

These external resources complement the tools above for serious students of the Greek New Testament.

  • Basics of Biblical Greek by William D. Mounce — the textbook these tools are built around. Widely used in seminary Greek courses.
  • Blue Letter Bible Lexicon — free online Greek lexicon with Strong’s numbers, parsing data, and usage statistics for every NT word.
  • BDAG Lexicon — the standard scholarly Greek-English lexicon for the NT (Bauer, Danker, Arndt, Gingrich). Essential for serious exegesis.
  • Sonic Light / Constable’s Notes — free expository notes on every NT book with careful attention to the Greek text, from a Free Grace perspective.
  • Grace Evangelical Society — journal articles and resources on the Greek text with Free Grace exegetical commitments.
