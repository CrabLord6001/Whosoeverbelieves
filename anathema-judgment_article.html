The Anathema Judgment

8 Ἀλλὰ καὶ ἐὰν ἡμεῖς ἢ ἄγγελος ἐξ οὐρανοῦ εὐαγγελίζηται ὑμῖν παρʼ ὃ εὐηγγελισάμεθα ὑμῖν, ἀνάθεμα ἔστω.

9 Ὡς προειρήκαμεν, καὶ ἄρτι πάλιν λέγω, εἴ τις ὑμᾶς εὐαγγελίζεται παρʼ ὃ παρελάβετε, ἀνάθεμα ἔστω.

In the LXX, the term ἀνάθεμα is used in place of חֵרֶם (ḥērem) that generally means marked out for physical destruction. For instance, Joshua 6:17–18 describes the city of Jericho as “devoted” to destruction. Likewise, Deuteronomy 7:26 declares Israelites who horde idols will suffer ruin in the same manner as the idols themselves. The accursed state of objects results in their temporal destruction.

The same divine ordered destruction concept applies in the New Testament. In Acts 23:14, the Jewish adversaries of Paul had bound themselves under a curse (solemn oath to die) until they kill him, using the closely related noun ἀναθέματι. Paul uses it similarly to call forth divine judgment in this temporal realm (1 Cor. 12:3, 16:22). Romans 9:3 uniquely applies it as a hyperbole for Paul’s wish that he might be accursed from Christ in exchange for Israel’s redemption, echoing Moses’ plea in Exodus 32:32.

In Galatians 1:8-9, Paul gives the imperative command for the readers to pronounce anathema on anyone who distorts the gospel of Jesus. It is unfortunately the NIV 1984 edition rendered it “eternally condemned”.

First, Paul includes himself and the other apostles in the potential curse. If he thought he could lose salvation, he would contradict numerous other Scriptures.

Anyone teaching a different gospel should be treated as a contamination to church unity. Just as Israel needed to ban unclean objects and food, the Church ought to strictly exclude false teachers. They are severed from experiencing blessing in Christ (Gal. 5:4).
